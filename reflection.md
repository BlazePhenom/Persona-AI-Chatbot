# Reflection

This project made the GIGO principle painfully clear. The quality of the system prompts directly shaped how authentic the personas felt. When the prompt carried concrete, sourced facts and a specific teaching style, the replies sounded like a real mentor. When a section was vague, responses became generic. The difference was immediate and obvious. The most useful upgrade was shifting from generic "be helpful" language to explicit behavior: how they explain, how long they respond, how they end with a question, and which topics they prioritize.

What worked best was writing prompts as mini playbooks. I listed the background, values, and voice first, then embedded few-shot examples that mirrored real student questions. That alignment helped the model stay on track without drifting into a bland assistant tone. The few-shot examples were also a natural guardrail against overlong answers and off-topic tangents. I also learned that constraints are not just safety requirements but style controls. Clear instructions like "do not claim private knowledge" and "do not reveal reasoning" preserved realism and trust.

Research mattered more than expected. Pulling facts from Scaler's About page, the IIIT Hyderabad alumni article, and public bios gave the prompts a grounded core. For Kshitij, the Scaler Academy brochure provided the specific career and education details that otherwise would have been guessed. That specificity is the difference between a believable instructor and a generic tutor. It also reminded me to avoid putting words into a real person's mouth; I kept to verified themes and used the persona voice to interpret, not invent.

If I had more time, I would deepen the data for each persona by collecting more direct quotes and class snippets, then refine the prompts to match their vocabulary and pacing. I would also add a tiny feedback loop in the UI that lets users rate responses, which would help measure whether the persona voice feels authentic. Finally, I would add a lightweight prompt testing harness so I could A/B test tone and format changes quickly. Overall, the biggest takeaway is that prompt quality is a design problem, not just a technical one, and every detail in the prompt acts like a product decision.
